Refrigeration Systems

Refrigeration Systems

Comprehensive coverage of refrigeration cycles, components, refrigerants, and applications spanning food preservation, cold storage, commercial refrigeration, and industrial processes. Refrigeration systems remove heat from low-temperature spaces and reject it to higher-temperature sinks through thermodynamic cycles.

Fundamental Cycles

Vapor Compression Refrigeration

The dominant refrigeration technology for commercial and industrial applications. Mechanical compression raises refrigerant pressure and temperature, enabling heat rejection at ambient conditions.

Key topics:

  • Cycle Analysis: P-h diagrams, COP calculations, thermodynamic processes
  • Superheat and Subcooling: Optimizing refrigeration effect
  • Multi-Stage Compression: Low-temperature applications
  • Cascade Systems: Ultra-low temperature refrigeration

Alternative Cycles

Non-vapor compression technologies for specific applications where electricity or mechanical drive is unavailable or inefficient.

Technologies:

  • Absorption Refrigeration: Waste heat or gas-fired systems
  • Adsorption Refrigeration: Solar cooling applications
  • Gas Cycle (Air Cycle): Aircraft environmental control
  • Magnetic Refrigeration: Emerging technology
  • Thermoelectric: Small-scale cooling
  • Stirling Cycle: Research applications

Refrigerants

Classification and Properties

Refrigerant selection impacts performance, safety, environmental compliance, and operating costs. Modern refrigerants balance thermodynamic properties with low global warming potential (GWP) and zero ozone depletion potential (ODP).

Refrigerant families:

  • CFCs (R-11, R-12): Banned (high ODP)
  • HCFCs (R-22): Phase-out complete (ODP > 0)
  • HFCs (R-134a, R-404A, R-410A): Current standard (GWP restrictions)
  • HFOs (R-1234yf, R-1234ze): Low-GWP future refrigerants
  • Natural Refrigerants (R-717, R-744, R-290): Zero GWP alternatives

System Components

Compressors

Compressors raise refrigerant pressure and temperature, driving the refrigeration cycle. Compressor type selection depends on capacity, refrigerant, and application.

Types:

  • Reciprocating: 1-200 hp, wide capacity range
  • Scroll: 1-50 hp, quiet, efficient for A/C
  • Screw: 20-1,000+ hp, industrial applications
  • Centrifugal: 100-10,000+ tons, large chillers

Performance factors:

  • Volumetric efficiency
  • Isentropic efficiency
  • Capacity modulation (steps, VFD, slide valve)

Related Technical Guides:

  • Compressor Selection & Performance

Heat Exchangers

Evaporators: Absorb heat from refrigerated space

  • Shell-and-tube
  • Plate heat exchangers
  • Finned-tube coils
  • Flooded vs. direct expansion (DX)

Condensers: Reject heat to ambient

  • Air-cooled (finned-tube)
  • Water-cooled (shell-and-tube)
  • Evaporative condensers

Related Technical Guides:

  • Evaporator Design
  • Condenser Design

Expansion Devices

Function: Reduce refrigerant pressure from condenser to evaporator

Types:

  • Thermostatic Expansion Valve (TXV): Superheat control
  • Electronic Expansion Valve (EEV): Precise control
  • Capillary Tube: Fixed orifice, small systems
  • Float Valves: Flooded evaporators

Applications

Commercial Refrigeration

Supermarkets, convenience stores, restaurants require reliable refrigeration for food safety and merchandising.

Systems:

  • Display Cases: Open, glass-door, reach-in
  • Walk-in Coolers and Freezers: Custom and prefabricated
  • Refrigerated Warehouses: Large-scale cold storage
  • Ice Machines: Flake, cube, specialty ice

Common refrigerants: R-404A (being replaced), R-448A, R-449A, R-744 (CO₂)

Industrial Refrigeration

Food processing, chemical plants, and cold storage facilities use large-capacity ammonia or CO₂ systems.

Characteristics:

  • High efficiency (ammonia COP 4-6)
  • Large capacity (hundreds to thousands of tons)
  • Centralized engine rooms
  • Sophisticated controls and safety systems

Common refrigerants: R-717 (ammonia), R-744 (CO₂ cascade)

Food Processing and Storage

Temperature control preserves food quality, prevents spoilage, and ensures food safety.

Temperature ranges:

  • Refrigerated storage: 32-50°F (fruits, vegetables, dairy, meat)
  • Frozen storage: -10 to +10°F (frozen foods, ice cream)
  • Blast freezing: -40 to -20°F (rapid freezing)
  • Ultra-low temperature: -40 to -100°F (seafood, specialty products)

Commodity-specific requirements:

  • Fruits and Vegetables: Controlled atmosphere, ethylene control
  • Meat and Poultry: Aging, chill/freeze cycles, humidity control
  • Dairy: Pasteurization, aging (cheese), ice cream hardening
  • Seafood: Brine freezing, plate freezing, glazing

Transport Refrigeration

Mobile refrigeration maintains temperature during shipping.

Applications:

  • Refrigerated trucks (reefers): Road transport
  • Refrigerated railcars: Long-distance shipping
  • Refrigerated containers: Intermodal shipping
  • Marine refrigeration: Ships, cargo holds

Food Properties and Refrigeration Loads

Thermal Properties

Accurate thermal property data enables load calculations and equipment sizing.

Key properties:

  • Specific heat: Above and below freezing point
  • Latent heat of fusion: Energy to freeze water content
  • Thermal conductivity: Heat transfer through product
  • Density: Mass per unit volume

Cooling and freezing time calculations determine process equipment sizing.

Refrigeration Load Components

  1. Product load: Cooling from initial to final temperature
  2. Respiration load: Heat generated by living produce
  3. Infiltration load: Warm air entering through doors
  4. Transmission load: Heat gain through walls, ceiling, floor
  5. Internal load: Lights, fans, forklifts, personnel
